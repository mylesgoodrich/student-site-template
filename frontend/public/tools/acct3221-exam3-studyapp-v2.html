CH 15 Entities Overview — Legal Classifications & Double Taxation
▼
Entity Comparison

Sole Proprietorship

  • Schedule C (Form 1040)
  • No liability protection
  • Income is always SE income
  • Flow-through taxation
  • Cannot issue stock / no IPO

Partnership / LLC

  • Form 1065 + Schedule K-1
  • LP: limited partners protected
  • LLC: members protected
  • Flow-through — no entity tax
  • SE tax depends on involvement

C Corporation

  • Form 1120 — separate taxpayer
  • Full shareholder liability protection
  • 21% flat corporate rate
  • Double taxation
  • Can issue stock / eligible for IPO
🔑
Check-the-Box Regulations: LLCs can elect their tax classification. Single-member LLC → disregarded entity (Schedule C). Multi-member LLC → default partnership. Can elect to be taxed as a C corp or S corp by filing Form 8832.
Double Taxation & Mitigation Strategies

C corps face two layers of tax: (1) 21% corporate tax on taxable income, and (2) individual shareholder tax when dividends are distributed (0%, 15%, or 20% + potential 3.8% NIIT).

QBI Deduction & Special Taxes

Flow-through owners may deduct 20% of Qualified Business Income (QBI) — a below-the-line (from AGI) deduction. NOT available for specified service trades or businesses (SSTBs): law, accounting, health, consulting, etc.

💰
Self-Employment Tax: 15.3% on first $176,100 of net SE earnings (0.9235 × gross SE). Sole props and general partners = always SE. S corp allocations = NOT SE (key planning tool). Deduct 50% of SE tax for AGI.
⚠️
NIIT (3.8%): Applies when modified AGI exceeds $250,000 MFJ / $200,000 single. Passive income from S corps and partnerships IS investment income for NIIT. Active S corp wages are NOT.
Deducting Entity Losses (Entity Selection Consideration)

C corp losses (NOLs) provide NO immediate benefit to shareholders. Flow-through entity losses can offset owners' other income, subject to at-risk, passive activity, and basis limitations. This makes flow-through entities attractive for start-up businesses expecting early losses.

CH 16 Corporate Operations — Income Tax Formula & Book-Tax Differences

Corporate Tax Formula Differences from Individuals
  • No AGI concept → no for-AGI or from-AGI deductions
  • No standard or itemized deductions
  • No personal/dependency exemptions
  • No QBI deduction for corporations
  • Flat 21% rate on all taxable income
  • Must use accrual method if average gross receipts > $30 million (3-year test)
📋
Deduction Sequencing Order: (1) All other deductions → (2) Charitable Contributions (10% limit) → (3) Dividends Received Deduction → (4) NOL deduction

CH 18 Nonliquidating Distributions — E&P, Dividends, Return of Capital
▼
Distribution Waterfall — The 3 Buckets
§301 Distribution Characterization
Step 1: Dividend IncomeTo the extent of current + accumulated E&P
Step 2: Return of CapitalExcess over E&P, reduces shareholder's stock basis (not income)
Step 3: Capital GainIf distribution exceeds both E&P AND stock basis → capital gain
🔑
Current vs. Accumulated E&P: Current year E&P is allocated pro rata across distributions during the year. Accumulated E&P from prior years is applied in order of distributions. A deficit in accumulated E&P does NOT offset current E&P. Current E&P can be negative (deficit) and reduces the dividend characterization.
Noncash Property Distributions

When a corporation distributes appreciated property (not cash), the corporation MUST recognize gain as if it sold the property at FMV on the distribution date. This gain increases E&P before determining the dividend amount.

  • Corporation recognizes gain = FMV − adjusted basis (but NOT loss on distribution)
  • E&P is increased by the gain recognized, then reduced by the FMV of the property distributed
  • Shareholder's amount received = FMV of property
  • Shareholder's basis in received property = FMV (regardless of corp's basis)
⚠️
Exam Trap: Corporations CANNOT recognize losses on nonliquidating distributions of property. Only gains are recognized. This is different from liquidating distributions where losses can sometimes be recognized.
Constructive Dividends

The IRS can recharacterize as dividends transactions that appear to be something else but economically benefit shareholders. Common examples include: excessive compensation to shareholder-employees, below-market loans, personal expenses paid by the corp, rent paid for shareholder's personal property, and corporate payment of shareholder's personal liabilities.

⚠️
Constructive dividends are taxable to the shareholder but NOT deductible to the corporation. The IRS raises constructive dividend assertions most commonly in closely-held C corporations where the controlling shareholder sets their own salary.
CH 19 §351 Corporate Formation, Taxable Acquisitions & Liquidations
▼
§351 — Three Requirements for Tax Deferral

1. Property

  • Money, tangible/intangible assets qualify
  • Services do NOT count as property
  • Services + property = qualifies if property ≥ 10% of services FMV
  • Investment companies excluded

2. Stock Only

  • Must receive ONLY stock in exchange
  • Receiving boot (cash/non-stock property) triggers partial gain recognition
  • Applies to both C and S corps
  • Losses are NEVER recognized under §351

3. Control (≥80%)

  • Must own ≥80% voting stock
  • Must own ≥80% all other classes
  • Measured IMMEDIATELY after exchange
  • Transfers need not be simultaneous
  • New shareholder: must transfer FMV ≥ 10% of existing interest
The 5-Step §351 Framework
Step-by-Step Computation
Step 1: Realized Gain/LossFMV of stock + Boot received + Liability relief − Adjusted basis of property
Step 2: Recognized GainLESSER of: (a) Realized Gain or (b) FMV of Boot received
Note: Losses are NEVER recognized under §351
Step 3: Unrecognized GainStep 1 − Step 2 (used in basis calc)
Step 4: Character of GainDepends on type of property transferred (capital, ordinary, §1231)
Step 5a: Shareholder's Stock BasisFMV of stock − Unrecognized gain (Shortcut) OR: Old basis + Gain recognized − Boot received − Liability relief
Step 5b: Corporation's Asset BasisShareholder's old basis + Gain recognized by shareholder
🔑
Liability Assumption — §357 Rules: General rule: liabilities assumed by corp are NOT boot (no gain triggered). Two exceptions: §357(b) — personal debt or tax avoidance purpose → ALL liability relief is boot. §357(c) — total liabilities exceed total basis of all property transferred → EXCESS is boot (gain recognized). §357(b) supersedes §357(c) if both apply.

Corporate Liquidations

Shareholder level: Treated as if shareholder sold stock. Amount realized = FMV of property received − liabilities. Gain/loss = Amount realized − adjusted basis in stock. Property received takes FMV as new basis.

Corporation level: Treated as if property was sold at FMV. Gains recognized; losses may be disallowed (related party/anti-stuffing rules). Exception: 80%+ corporate shareholder → neither gain nor loss recognized (§332 liquidation); carryover basis in property.

CH 20 Partnership Formation & Operations — Inside/Outside Basis, K-1 Reporting
▼
Partnership Formation — Nonrecognition General Rule

Under §721, no gain or loss is recognized by either the partner or partnership when property is contributed to a partnership in exchange for a partnership interest. Key exception: services do not qualify for nonrecognition — the service provider must recognize ordinary income equal to the FMV of the capital interest received.

🔑
Capital Interest vs. Profits Interest: Capital interest (right to liquidation proceeds) — service partner reports ordinary income = FMV of interest received. Profits interest (only future profits; zero current liquidation value) — NO income recognized; zero basis.
💡
§721 Encourages Built-in Gains, Not Losses: Partners contribute appreciated property tax-free, but built-in losses don't help them immediately. The carryover basis rule means the loss is "trapped" in the partnership.
Inside Basis vs. Outside Basis

Inside basis = the partnership's tax basis in its assets. Outside basis = each partner's tax basis in their partnership interest. When there is no debt: Sum of all outside bases = Sum of inside bases (aggregate of all assets).

Outside Basis Calculation
+ Contributed property (carryover tax basis)
+ Share of recourse debt (partners with economic risk of loss)
+ Share of nonrecourse debt (pro rata profit sharing ratio)
− Debt relief (liabilities assumed by partnership reduces basis)
+ Gain recognized (if deemed distribution exceeds basis)
= Partner's Outside Basis in Partnership Interest
Debt Allocation Rules
Debt TypeAllocation RuleEffect on Outside Basis
Recourse debtAllocated to partners who bear economic risk of loss (usually general partners)Increases outside basis
Nonrecourse debtAllocated based on profit-sharing ratiosIncreases outside basis proportionally
Partnership assumes partner's debtTreated as a deemed cash distribution to the contributing partnerDecreases that partner's outside basis
⚠️
§752(b) Gain Recognition Trap: If a partnership assumes a partner's liability AND that deemed cash distribution EXCEEDS the partner's outside basis in the contributed property → partner must recognize gain. The gain is generally ordinary in character (same as the contributed property).
Holding Periods & Partnership Reporting
  • Partnership interest itself = capital asset
  • Capital and §1231 assets contributed: holding period tacks (starts from original acquisition date)
  • Otherwise: holding period begins the day the partnership interest is acquired
  • Partnership files Form 1065 (information return only — not a taxpaying entity)
  • Each partner receives Schedule K-1 reporting their share of separately stated items
🔑
Separately Stated Items must be reported apart from ordinary income if they could affect any partner's tax differently (e.g., capital gains/losses, charitable contributions, §1231 gains, qualified dividends). Guaranteed payments = ordinary income to recipient, deducted from partnership ordinary income, always self-employment income.
CH 22 S Corporations — Election, Requirements, Operations & Basis
▼
S Corporation Eligibility Requirements

Shareholder Restrictions

  • ≤ 100 shareholders maximum
  • Family members count as ONE shareholder
  • Must be individuals, estates, or certain trusts
  • NO corporations or partnerships as shareholders
  • Must be US citizens or residents

Corporate Restrictions

  • Domestic corporation only
  • Only ONE class of stock
  • Voting differences are OK (not different economic rights)
  • Cannot be an ineligible corporation type
  • All shares: identical distribution & liquidation rights
S Corp Election — Timing Rules (Critical for Exam!)
Election Effective Date
Effective current year IF filed:By 15th day of 3rd month of current tax year (March 15 for calendar year)
Effective NEXT year IF filed:After 15th day of 3rd month, or in prior tax year for next year
Required consent:ALL shareholders who owned stock during the election year must consent (Form 2553)
⚠️
Classic Exam Trap: Election made on March 20 for a calendar-year corp → effective January 1 of the NEXT year (after the March 15 deadline). Election made on February 20 → effective immediately (current year). ALL shareholders with any ownership during the election year must consent — not just current shareholders.
S Corp Termination Rules
TypeTriggerEffective DateRe-election Wait
Voluntary revocationMajority (>50%) of shareholders consentMarch 15 or earlier → 1st day of current year; after → 1st day of next year5 years (unless IRS consents earlier)
Involuntary — eligibility failureFails any S corp requirementDate of the failure5 years
Involuntary — passive incomePassive income >25% of gross receipts for 3 consecutive years AND has accumulated C corp E&PFirst day of tax year following the 3rd consecutive year5 years
💡
If corp was never a C corp OR has no accumulated C corp E&P → the passive income test can NEVER terminate the S election. IRS may consent to earlier re-election if the termination was inadvertent and the corporation takes remedial steps.
S Corp Operations & Basis
  • Tax year: Generally calendar year; must have business purpose for alternative year (e.g., 25% of gross receipts in last 2 months of proposed year-end)
  • Income allocation: MUST be pro rata based on shares outstanding each day (less flexible than partnerships)
  • No guaranteed payments or SE income from S corp allocations
  • S corp ordinary income: NOT self-employment income to shareholder-employees (key planning opportunity)
  • Must pay reasonable salary if shareholder works for S corp — IRS can reclassify distributions as wages
S Corp Shareholder Stock Basis (Similar to Partnership)
Initial basis= §351 rules (carryover basis / purchase price)
+ Separately stated income items
+ Ordinary business income
− Distributions
− Separately stated losses/deductions
− Ordinary business loss (limited to basis)
= Ending Stock Basis (cannot go below zero)
⚠️
Critical Difference from Partnerships: S corp shareholders CANNOT include S corp debt in their stock basis (unlike partners who can include partnership debt in outside basis). However, shareholders CAN get basis from direct loans they personally make to the S corp (debt basis — separate from stock basis).
🔑
C Corp NOLs Don't Transfer to S Corp: When converting from C to S corp, accumulated C corp NOLs cannot be used in S corp years. They can only be utilized if the corp converts back to C status.
CH 24 US Taxation of Multinational Transactions — Nexus, Residency, FTC
▼
The Multinational Framework

Outbound Transactions

  • US taxpayer earning foreign income
  • US taxes worldwide income
  • Foreign tax credit (FTC) mitigates double tax
  • Example: US corp with foreign subsidiary

Inbound Transactions

  • Foreign entity earning US-source income
  • Taxed only on US-source income
  • Withholding taxes on passive US income
  • Tax treaties can reduce rates

Foreign Tax Credit (FTC) — Mitigating Double Taxation

The FTC prevents double taxation by allowing US taxpayers to credit foreign taxes paid against their US tax liability. The credit is limited to the US tax that would have been paid on the foreign income.

FTC Limitation Formula
FTC Limitation =(Foreign source taxable income / Total taxable income) × Pre-credit US tax
Excess Credits:Carry back 1 year, carry forward 10 years (except GILTI)
🔑
4 FTC Baskets (calculated separately): (1) Global Intangible Low-Taxed Income (GILTI), (2) Foreign Branch Income, (3) Passive Category Income, (4) General Category Income. Separation prevents cross-crediting high-tax income to offset low-tax income limitations.

💡
Tax Treaties: US has income tax treaties with 66+ countries. Treaties: (1) define nexus/permanent establishment, (2) reduce or eliminate withholding taxes on cross-border interest, dividends, and royalties, (3) resolve residence conflicts. Treaties generally CANNOT increase tax above statutory rates.
