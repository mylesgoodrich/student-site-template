Chapter 15 — Entities Overview

Legal Entities & Liability Protection

Tax law starts with a legal classification, then layers on a tax classification via the check-the-box regulations. Liability protection is a nontax characteristic but drives entity choice.

EntityLiability Protection?Default Tax Classification
Sole Proprietorship❌ Unlimited personal liabilitySchedule C on 1040 (flow-through)
General Partnership❌ Unlimited for general partnersPartnership (Form 1065)
Limited Partnership✅ Limited partners only; ❌ GP unlimitedPartnership
LLC (multi-member)✅ All members protectedPartnership (default) — can elect corp
LLC (single-member)✅ Member protectedDisregarded entity — sole prop or corp
C Corporation✅ Shareholders protectedSeparate taxpayer (Form 1120)
S Corporation✅ Shareholders protectedFlow-through (Form 1120-S)
🔑 Key Rule LLCs deliver corporate-style liability protection with flow-through taxation — the best of both worlds, which is why they dominate small-business formations.

Check-the-Box Regulations

Eligible entities (non-corporations) may elect their tax classification on Form 8832. A corporation by state law (Inc., Corp.) is per se a corporation and cannot elect out.

Double Taxation — C Corporations

  • Layer 1: C corp pays 21% flat tax on taxable income.
  • Layer 2: Shareholders pay tax on dividends at 0/15/20% qualified dividend rates (individuals), plus potential 3.8% NIIT if modified AGI > $250K MFJ / $200K single.
  • Flow-through entities do NOT pay entity-level tax — owners pay once on their allocable share.

★ Strategies to Mitigate Double Taxation ★

These come up every exam. The C corp deducts these payments (reducing corporate-level tax), and the shareholder is taxed only once on receipt:

  • Pay salaries to shareholder-employees (deductible to corp, ordinary income to SH — must be "reasonable compensation" to survive IRS scrutiny)
  • Lease property from shareholders (rent deduction to corp, rental income to SH)
  • Loan money to the corporation (interest deduction to corp, interest income to SH)
  • Retain earnings (defer layer 2 indefinitely — watch out for §531 accumulated earnings tax)
  • Elect S corp status (eliminate layer 1 entirely)
⚠️ Exam Trap The IRS can reclassify "excessive" salary, rent, or interest as a constructive dividend — treated as a nondeductible dividend to the corp and ordinary dividend income to the SH, erasing the mitigation.

Qualified Business Income (QBI) Deduction — §199A

  • 20% deduction of QBI allocated from flow-through entities (from-AGI deduction)
  • Cannot be from a specified service trade or business (SSTB) — health, law, accounting, consulting, performing arts — above income thresholds
  • QBI excludes W-2 wages and investment income (capital gains, dividends, interest)

Self-Employment Tax — By Entity

Entity TypeSE Tax Treatment
Sole ProprietorshipIncome IS SE income (full 15.3% SE tax)
General PartnershipGP's share of ordinary income IS SE income
Limited PartnershipLP's share NOT SE (only guaranteed payments are)
LLC MemberDepends on involvement (generally yes if actively managing)
S CorporationShareholder's ordinary income NOT SE (W-2 wages are FICA-taxed)
💡 SE Tax Math 15.3% (12.4% SS + 2.9% Medicare) on first $176,100 of net SE earnings (2024/2025); 2.9% Medicare above that; extra 0.9% Medicare surtax above $250K MFJ / $200K single. Base = net SE earnings × 0.9235.

★ Deducting Entity Losses — Entity Selection ★

  • C corp losses: No immediate benefit — trapped at entity level as NOL. Only helps if/when the corp earns future income.
  • Flow-through losses: Pass to owners immediately and can offset other income (subject to basis, at-risk, passive-activity, and excess-business-loss limits).
🔑 Exam Guidance For a new business expecting early losses, a flow-through (LLC/partnership/S corp) is generally preferred so owners can deduct losses currently. A C corp would warehouse them.

★ Book-Tax Differences (BTDs) ★

  • Favorable vs. Unfavorable — does it decrease (F) or increase (U) taxable income vs. book income?

★ §197 Asset Acquisition Goodwill ★

  • Tax: Amortize over 15 years straight-line as §197 intangible
  • Book (GAAP): No amortization; only impairment
  • If tax amortization > book impairment → favorable temporary BTD
  • If book impairment > tax amortization → unfavorable temporary BTD
  • Stock acquisition goodwill: No tax amortization allowed → impairment creates unfavorable PERMANENT BTD

★ Dividends Received Deduction (DRD) ★

< 20%50%50% × 21% = 10.5%

📋 IRC §243 Purpose of DRD: mitigate triple taxation of earnings passed through a chain of C corps. DRD is a favorable permanent BTD.

NOL Rules (Post-2020)

  • No carryback for NOLs arising in tax years beginning after 12/31/2020
  • Carry forward indefinitely
  • Can only offset up to 80% of taxable income (computed before the NOL deduction)
  • Use on FIFO basis (oldest first)
  • Pre-2018 NOLs: 2-yr back, 20-yr forward, 100% of TI

Charitable Contributions — Corporate

  • Limited to 10% of adjusted taxable income (TI before charitable, DRD, NOL CB, and capital loss CB)
  • Excess carries forward 5 years (FIFO)
  • Accrual corps can elect to deduct contributions authorized by board before year-end if paid by April 15 (or 15th day of 4th month)

Deduction Sequencing (Critical)

1. Start with gross income minus ordinary deductions 2. Take Charitable Contribution deduction (10% limit) 3. Take DRD 4. Take NOL carryforward (80% limit) = Taxable Income × 21%

Chapter 18 — Nonliquidating Distributions

★ The Three-Tier Distribution Waterfall ★

Every corporate distribution is tested against this sequence. Memorize it.

Distribution → (1) Dividend to the extent of E&P (current + accumulated) (2) Return of capital to the extent of SH's stock basis (3) Capital gain to the extent distribution exceeds basis

Earnings & Profits (E&P)

  • E&P measures a corporation's economic earnings available for distribution — NOT the same as retained earnings.
  • Computed starting from taxable income with adjustments (add back DRD, municipal interest; subtract federal tax, nondeductible items).
  • Current E&P = current-year economic earnings. Accumulated E&P = prior-year cumulative.

Four E&P Scenarios for Classifying a Distribution

Current E&PAccum. E&PTreatment
PositivePositiveDividend to extent of sum
PositiveNegativeDividend to extent of current E&P only (current "nets" first)
NegativePositiveProrate current deficit to date of distribution; net against accumulated
NegativeNegativeEntire distribution = ROC, then cap gain
⚠️ Exam Trap Current E&P is tested first. Even with a huge deficit in accumulated E&P, a distribution up to current E&P is still a dividend. Example: Current +$120, Accum −$200, distribution $100 → fully dividend.

Noncash Property Distributions

To the shareholder:

  • Amount of distribution = FMV of property (reduced by liabilities assumed by SH, but not below zero)
  • SH's basis in received property = FMV (no downward adjustment for liabilities)
  • Classification (dividend / ROC / cap gain) follows the 3-tier waterfall

To the distributing corporation:

  • Gain recognized as if property sold at FMV (even though no sale occurred)
  • Loss NOT recognized on distribution
  • E&P increases by gain recognized; E&P decreases by greater of FMV or basis (reduced by liabilities)

★ Constructive Dividends ★

The IRS may assert a distribution exists even without a formal declaration when a C corp transfers value to a shareholder disguised as something else. Common triggers:

  • Excessive compensation to shareholder-employees
  • Below-market loans from corp to SH
  • Rent paid to SH in excess of FMV
  • SH use of corporate property (cars, homes, jets) without reimbursement
  • Unreasonably high interest paid on SH debt
  • Bargain sales of corporate property to SH
⚠️ Why This Matters Constructive dividends flip a deductible payment into a nondeductible dividend at the corp level — and the SH loses the ability to treat it as basis or wages. Double-tax whammy.

Chapter 19 — Corporate Formation & Liquidation

Why Defer? (Policy)

Congress enacted §351 so that taxpayers can incorporate a going concern without triggering tax on what is essentially a mere change in form. The shareholder's investment continues — just in corporate-stock form instead of directly in the assets. The deferred gain is preserved via carryover basis.

★ §351 — The Three Requirements ★

📋 IRC §351(a) "No gain or loss shall be recognized if property is transferred to a corporation by one or more persons solely in exchange for stock in such corporation and immediately after the exchange such person or persons are in control of the corporation."
  1. PROPERTY is transferred — money, tangible assets, intangibles (patents, goodwill). Services are NOT property. If a SH contributes only services for stock, that SH gets ordinary compensation income (FMV of stock) and is excluded from the control group.
  2. In exchange solely for STOCK of the transferee corporation (common or preferred; but not nonqualified preferred).
  3. Transferors as a group must have CONTROL (§368(c)) immediately after: ≥80% of voting stock AND ≥80% of each non-voting class.
💡 10% Rule for Services If a SH contributes BOTH property AND services for stock, the property portion is "not of relatively small value" and counts toward control only if its FMV is at least 10% of the FMV of services. Otherwise the SH is excluded from the control group.

★ 5-Step Method (from the §351 Handout) ★

  1. Realized gain/loss = (FMV of stock + boot + liability relief) − basis of property contributed
  2. Recognized gain = LESSER of realized gain OR boot received. Losses never recognized in §351.
  3. Unrecognized gain = realized − recognized (used for basis)
  4. Character of recognized gain = character of property contributed (capital, §1231, ordinary)
  5. Basis of stock received (two methods):
Code Section Method (Shareholder stock basis): Adjusted basis of property contributed + Gain recognized − FMV of boot received − Liabilities assumed by corporation = SH's basis in stock received Shortcut Method: FMV of stock received − Unrecognized gain (OR + unrecognized loss) = SH's basis in stock received Corporation's basis in property received: Transferor's adjusted basis + Gain recognized by transferor = Corp's basis (carryover + step-up for gain)

★ Boot Rules ★

  • Boot = anything received other than stock of the transferee corp (cash, promissory notes, other property, excess liability relief)
  • Boot does NOT disqualify §351 — it just triggers partial recognition
  • Gain recognized = lesser of realized gain OR FMV of boot
  • No losses are ever recognized, even with boot

★ §357 — Assumption of Liabilities ★

  • General rule §357(a): Liability assumption is NOT boot
  • Exception §357(b): If any liability was incurred for tax avoidance or non-business purpose → ALL liabilities assumed become boot
  • Exception §357(c): If total liabilities assumed > total basis of property contributed → excess = recognized gain
  • If both apply: §357(b) supersedes §357(c).
⚠️ §357(c) Gain This is the one real "gotcha" of incorporation. If you contribute land (basis $40K) with a mortgage ($100K), you recognize $60K gain under §357(c) even though you wanted full deferral.

Worked Example — Al Pine Incorporation

Contributes inventory/building/land: FMV $375K, basis $175K, liability $75K. Receives only stock (FMV $300K).

Step 1: Realized gain = ($300K stock + $75K liability) − $175K basis = $200K Step 2: Recognized gain = $0 (no boot; liability ≤ basis, so §357(c) doesn't apply) Step 3: Unrecognized gain = $200K Step 5 (shortcut): Stock basis = $300K FMV − $200K unrecognized = $100K Corp's basis in property = $175K + $0 gain = $175K

🔑 Why Buyers Prefer Asset Acquisitions Step-up basis → more depreciation/amortization deductions. Goodwill becomes amortizable. Avoid inheriting unknown liabilities. Sellers often prefer stock deals (single level of tax), creating negotiating tension.

Corporate Liquidations (§331, §336, §337)

To the shareholder (§331):

  • Treated as if stock were sold — amount realized (FMV of property − liabilities) vs. stock basis
  • Gain or loss recognized; capital in character (stock is capital asset)
  • Basis in property received = FMV on distribution date
  • Exception §332: 80% corporate parent recognizes no gain/loss; takes carryover basis

To the liquidating corporation (§336):

  • Treated as if it sold property at FMV
  • Gains recognized; losses may be disallowed (related-party rules under §336(d))
  • §337: No gain/loss on distributions to 80% corporate parent

Chapter 20 — Partnership Formation & Operations

Flow-Through Taxation: Entity vs. Aggregate

Partnerships mix two theories:

  • Entity approach: Partnership files Form 1065, maintains books, owns assets — treated as separate entity for reporting.
  • Aggregate approach: Income, character, and basis flow through to partners as if each directly owned a share of assets.

★ §721 — Partnership Formation ★

Analog to §351 but simpler — no control requirement, no 80% threshold.

📋 IRC §721(a) No gain or loss shall be recognized to a partnership or to any of its partners in the case of a contribution of property to the partnership in exchange for an interest in the partnership.
  • No gain or loss recognized on contribution of property
  • Applies at formation AND thereafter (unlike §351's "immediately after" rule)
  • Exception 1: Contribution of services in exchange for a capital interest → ordinary compensation income at FMV
  • Exception 2: §752 deemed distribution — if debt relief exceeds contributing partner's basis, recognize gain
  • §721(b) Exception: Contribution to investment partnership → taxable

★ Inside vs. Outside Basis ★

Inside BasisOutside Basis
Partnership's basis in its assetsPartner's basis in her partnership interest
Carryover from contributing partnerContributed property basis + gain recognized + debt share
Used for partnership's depreciation, gain on saleUsed for loss limits, gain on sale of interest, distribution rules
🔑 Equation When no debt is involved: Inside basis = Outside basis. This ensures gain is only taxed once.

★ §752 — Debt Allocation ★

TypeDefinitionAllocated To
RecoursePartner bears economic risk of lossPartner with ultimate responsibility (usually general partners)
NonrecoursePartnership secured only by collateralAll partners based on profit-sharing ratios
💡 Debt Rules
  • Increase in partner's share of debt = deemed cash contribution → ↑ outside basis
  • Decrease in partner's share of debt = deemed cash distribution → ↓ outside basis
  • LLC "general" debt (no personal guarantee) defaults to nonrecourse

★ Outside Basis Formula ★

Initial outside basis on formation: Cash contributed + Adjusted basis of property contributed + Services: ordinary income recognized + Share of partnership debt (recourse + nonrecourse) − Debt of contributor assumed by partnership (deemed distribution) + Gain recognized (if §752 deemed distribution > property basis) = Outside basis Annual adjustments: + Share of ordinary income + separately stated income/gains + Share of tax-exempt income + Additional contributions + debt increases − Share of ordinary loss + separately stated loss items − Share of nondeductible expenses − Distributions + debt decreases

Holding Period — Tacking Rules

  • Partner's interest: Tacks if capital or §1231 asset was contributed; otherwise begins on acquisition date
  • Partnership's holding period in contributed property: Always tacks (character does NOT tack — character is determined at partnership level based on use)

Services for Partnership Interest

TypeOrdinary Income?Basis in InterestPartnership Effect
Capital interest (right to liquidation proceeds)✅ Yes, at FMV= ordinary income recognizedDeduction (or capitalize) by non-service partners
Profits interest (future profits only)❌ No (no liquidation value)$0None

★ Form 1065 Reporting ★

  • Form 1065: Partnership information return (not a tax liability return)
  • Schedule K-1: Each partner's share of income, deductions, credits
  • Ordinary business income: Line 1 — net of partnership operations
  • Separately stated items: Items that could affect partners differently (capital gains, §1231 gains, charitable contributions, dividends, interest income, §179, foreign taxes, etc.)
📋 "Separately Stated" Rule An item must be separately stated if it could cause any partner's tax liability to differ from what it would be if partnership income were simply lumped together. Character follows each item through to the partner.

Guaranteed Payments

  • Fixed amounts paid to a partner regardless of partnership income/loss
  • Deducted in computing partnership ordinary income
  • Separately stated on K-1 to recipient
  • Ordinary income to receiving partner AND always SE income

Start-Up & Organization Costs

  • Expense up to $5,000 immediately (each category separately)
  • $5,000 phased out dollar-for-dollar as costs exceed $50,000
  • Amortize remainder over 180 months
  • Syndication costs: Never deductible (capitalized permanently)

Chapter 22 — S Corporations

Why S Corps Are Hybrid Creatures

S corps borrow heavily from both C corp and partnership rules:

  • From C corp law: state-law formation, liability protection, §351 formation rules, stock basis concepts
  • From partnership law: flow-through taxation, separately stated items, basis adjustments for income/loss

★ S Corp Shareholder & Corporate Requirements ★

RequirementRule
Entity typeDomestic corporation (or LLC electing corp status)
Max # of shareholders100 (family members treated as 1)
Eligible shareholdersIndividuals (US citizens/residents), estates, certain trusts (QSSTs, ESBTs), qualified retirement plans/501(c)(3)s
Ineligible shareholders❌ Partnerships, ❌ C corps, ❌ nonresident aliens
Classes of stockOne class only (differences in voting rights OK; differences in distribution/liquidation rights kill the election)
Shareholder consentALL shareholders on election date must consent

★ Election Timing (Form 2553) ★

🔑 The 2½-Month Rule Election must be filed by the 15th day of the 3rd month of the tax year to be effective that year. Late-filed elections become effective the following year. All shareholders who held stock at any point during the year of election must consent (even those who sold before the filing date).

Termination of S Election

Voluntary revocation: Requires consent of shareholders holding >50% of stock.

Involuntary termination: Triggered automatically by:

  • Failing any S corp requirement (e.g., issuing 101st stock certificate, transfer to nonresident alien)
  • Excessive passive investment income — >25% for 3 consecutive years when corp has C corp E&P
⚠️ 5-Year Waiting Period After termination, corporation must wait 5 years before re-electing S status (unless IRS grants early consent for inadvertent terminations).

Required Tax Year

  • Default: calendar year
  • May elect a natural business year end (§444) or demonstrate business purpose

★ S Corp Basis — Critical Differences from Partnerships ★

S Corp Stock BasisPartnership Outside Basis
Initial basisLike §351 — contributed basis + gain recognized − bootContributed basis + debt share
Entity debt included?❌ NEVER✅ Yes (recourse + nonrecourse shares)
Separate basis for loans to entity?✅ Yes — debt basis if SH directly lendsN/A (built into outside basis)
Annual adjustmentsLike partnership (income ↑, loss ↓, distributions ↓)Same
⚠️ THE Biggest S-Corp Exam Point An S corp shareholder gets NO basis from corporate-level debt — even if they personally guaranteed it! To get debt basis, the shareholder must directly loan money to the corporation. This is the opposite of partnership rules.

Loss Limitations — 4 Gatekeepers

  1. Tax basis (stock basis, then debt basis if applicable)
  2. At-risk amount (§465)
  3. Passive activity rules (§469)
  4. Excess business loss limitation (§461(l)) — $305K single / $610K MFJ (2025)

Operating Distributions — S Corp with No C Corp E&P

  • Tax-free to extent of stock basis (like partnership)
  • Excess over basis = capital gain
  • No E&P concept when S corp was never a C corp

SE Tax — S Corp Advantage

  • S corp ordinary income passing through to SH is NOT SE income (huge advantage vs. partnership GP)
  • SH-employees pay FICA on reasonable W-2 wages only
  • No guaranteed payments in S corps (partnership concept only)

Chapter 24 — US Taxation of Multinational Transactions

★ Key Terms ★

  • Nexus: Minimum connection required for a jurisdiction to impose tax. The core question in all cross-border tax.
  • Outbound transactions: US person earning income abroad (US-based Nike selling in Germany)
  • Inbound transactions: Foreign person earning income in the US (Toyota Japan selling cars in US)
  • Source: Jurisdiction based on where income is earned (territorial concept)
  • Residence: Jurisdiction based on who earned it (citizenship-based concept)

★ Worldwide vs. Territorial Systems ★

Worldwide (Residence) SystemTerritorial (Source) System
Tax baseALL income of residents, wherever earnedOnly income earned within borders
Used byUS (partial — since TCJA mixed)Most OECD countries (UK, Germany, Japan)
Double-tax riskHigh — income hit by source country AND home countryLow — only source country taxes
Relief mechanismForeign tax credit, treatiesGenerally none needed
🔑 CORE RULE US citizens and US residents are taxed on their WORLDWIDE income — regardless of where earned or where they live. Unique to US among developed nations.

★ Mitigating Double Taxation — Three Tools ★

  1. Foreign Tax Credit (FTC):
    • Dollar-for-dollar credit against US tax for foreign income taxes paid
    • Limitation formula: (Foreign source TI ÷ Total TI) × Precredit US tax
    • Computed separately for 4 baskets: GILTI, foreign branch, passive, general
    • Excess FTC carries back 1 year, forward 10 years (except GILTI — no carryover)
  2. Tax Treaties:
    • Bilateral agreements (US has ~66 in force) that define nexus and reduce withholding on cross-border payments
    • Common reductions: withholding on dividends, interest, royalties (default 30% → often reduced to 15% or lower)
  3. Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (§911): Up to ~$130K (2025) for US citizens living abroad meeting bona fide residence or physical presence tests

Anti-Deferral Regimes (Why the US Doesn't Just Lose Revenue)

  • Subpart F: Certain "tainted" passive-type income of Controlled Foreign Corporations (CFCs) is taxed to US shareholders immediately, even if not repatriated. Includes interest, dividends, rents, royalties, certain sales income, FX gains.
  • GILTI (§951A): Global Intangible Low-Taxed Income — current taxation of CFC earnings above a 10% return on tangible assets. Added by TCJA 2017.
  • BEAT (§59A): Base Erosion and Anti-Abuse Tax — min tax on large corporations that make deductible payments to foreign affiliates.
  • FDII (§250): Reduced rate (13.125%) on Foreign-Derived Intangible Income — incentive to keep IP in the US.
📋 Policy Summary TCJA 2017 shifted the US toward a quasi-territorial system (via §245A dividends-received deduction for 10%+ owned foreign subs) while adding GILTI/BEAT anti-abuse provisions. The net effect: participation exemption for active foreign income, current tax on mobile/passive income.
